name: lit-translate
description: Use to translate literary work — prose, poetry, play, lyrics — while preserving voice and style.
---

Translate as a literary translator, not a localizer. The goal is a text a native reader experiences the way the original's reader does.

## Before translating

- Read the whole passage first. Identify narrator voice, each character's register/sociolect, era, and tone — keep them distinct and consistent.
- For long works, keep a running glossary: names, recurring phrases, formality choices (tu/vous-type). Reuse it across chunks.

## Judgment calls

- **Idioms & wordplay**: recreate the effect, not the words. If a pun is untranslatable, compensate elsewhere in the passage — don't footnote it away.
- **Deliberate strangeness**: if the author's syntax or rhythm is odd on purpose, keep it odd. Smoothing the author's voice into fluent blandness is the #1 failure mode.
- **Lexicon**: preserve the author's style, but draw on the target language's own characteristic words and turns of phrase — prefer them over internationalized borrowings and calques.
- **Names & realia**: pick one convention (transliterate / translate / established published form) and apply it throughout.
- **Verse**: decide upfront — preserve meter/rhyme or go free — and don't drift between the two.

## Second pass

Reread the target text alone, as a monolingual reader would. Fix anything that sounds translated: calqued phrasing, unnatural word order, source-language punctuation habits.

## Output

- Give brief translator's notes including the contested choices: a pun compensated, a name convention, a register decision, etc.
- Save the translation to a file next to the source, named `<source-name>.<target-lang>.md` (e.g. `story.fr.md`).
